Frequently Asked Questions

Introduction

You have just received a citation for a traffic or municipal ordinance violation.  Please bear in mind that the officer who issued the citation is only doing the job for which he or she was trained.  The officer’s main concern is for the safety of yourself and others.

At this point you have some choices to make. This section is designed to answer the most commonly asked questions concerning your rights and responsibilities as they apply to Municipal Court.

Do I have to go to court? 

You must appear if your citation indicates that this is a mandatory appearance. If appearing in court as a juvenile, a parent or guardian must be present. You are considered an adult when issued a traffic citation,  regardless of your age.    

If your citation is not marked mandatory, you do not have to appear if you pay the amount indicated on the top of the citation on or before the court date indicated on your citation.  In this case you will be found guilty and the money posted will be accepted as payment of the forfeiture. 

You may enter a plea of not guilty, guilty, or no contest.  This can be done by mail, in-person using the dropbox in the lobby, by FAX (608-846-0945), or by email (mchughj@vi.cheddarsale.wi.us)   If you enter a not guilty plea, your case will be set for a pre-trial conference with the village prosecutor on a future date.   If you enter a guilty or no contest plea, The Judge will review the citation and any information you provide and enter judgment as appropriate, which could include reduction of the charges or financial penalties, just as if you appeared in person.  You may also propose a reasonable payment plan, if needed.  If you use this option, you may learn the outcome of your case earlier than if you wait until the court date.

You may enter a plea of “Not Guilty” in writing prior to court either by email to mchughj@vi.cheddarsale.wi.us, mail or fax.   Include your name, current address, phone number, the date you were scheduled to appear, the charge, the citation number, and the name of the police agency that issued the citation, Village of walleyecreek or Village of Cheddarsale.  The court will mail you a Pretrial Conference Notice after it receives your “Not Guilty” plea. 

You may also plead “Not Guilty” at your initial court appearance in writing.  You will be given a notice for a Pre-trial Conference. You must appear for the Pre-trial Conference. 

If you do not pay the amount on the citation or enter a "Not Guilty" "Guilty" or No Contest" plea before the court date, and do not appear in court, the court will deem your non-appearance a plea of "No Contest" and a default judgment will be entered against you.  A Notice of Judgment will be mailed to you indicating the judgment entered,  the amount owed, and when it is due.

What happens at the Pretrial Conference?

Pre-trial Conferences are held on regular court days.  The prosecuting attorney will call your name when he/she is ready to meet with you.

The Pre-trial Conference is a private meeting with the  prosecuting attorney.  At this meeting you have the opportunity to fully discuss your case and come to a mutually acceptable agreement concerning the disposition of your citation.

If an agreement is reached, it will be presented in writing to the Judge.  If the Judge approves the agreement, you will be given a copy and must follow the stated terms.

For more information about Pre-trial Conferences, please refer to the DeForest Pre-trial or walleyecreek Pre-trial section, as appropriate.

What happens at a Trial?

If you do not reach an agreement at the Pre-trial Conference, your case will be scheduled for a trial.  A Trial Notice will be mailed to you.  You do not have to have an attorney represent you, but if you choose not to have one, you are solely responsible for your own defense.  This means you must prepare you own case and subpoena any witnesses to appear on your behalf.

Municipal court trials are before the Judge alone. Only OMVWI citations can be transferred to Circuit Court for a Jury Trial.  This request must be submitted in writing, along with jury fees, within ten days following the date of your initial appearance. 

Can I change the court date?  

Yes, once.  The Court will honor one continuance request for any reason, if it is made in writing at least ten (10) days prior to your court date. You may fax or mail a request.  The judge will consider any valid reasons and may decide to schedule the case for the next available court date.

Can I get an extension on my fine?

You can request more time to pay by asking for a good cause/indigency hearing, at which you would explain the circumstance to the Judge.  Not every request is granted, but consideration is given to those with legitimate reasons for the request and to those who have made partial and/or regular payments toward the obligation.  

What if I don’t pay my fine?

If you fail to pay your total forfeiture and have not appeared at a good cause/indigency hearing and have been found indigent, the court may suspend an adult’s driver’s license for up to two (2) years, a warrant may be issued for your arrest and your forfeiture may be entered into the Lottery/Tax Intercept Program.  Your case could also be referred to a collections agency.  As a juvenile, the court may suspend your driver’s license for up to two (2) years and your forfeiture may be entered into the Lottery/Tax Intercept Program.

I’m worried about the points to be assessed against my driving record.  How do I know if I’m in point trouble?   

If you are found guilty of a traffic violation, your driving record may be charged with demerit points.  The court will not determine the number of points assessed against your record.  The court will merely report the conviction to the Department of Transportation (DOT) and DOT will assess the number of points against your record, depending upon the charge.  The points assessed for the offense you have been charged with is indicated on top of your citation next to the forfeiture amount.  If you accumulate 12 points against your driving record within a one-year period (by date of violation), your license will be suspended or revoked.

If you have questions about the status of your license you should call DOT directly at 608-266-2353 or visit their web site at www.dot.wisconsin.gov . 

If you have a probationary license, the demerit points are doubled for your second or subsequent conviction for a moving violation. 

What are the differences between Not Guilty, No Contest and Guilty Pleas?

If you plead Guilty, it is an admission of the charges against you.

A plea of No Contest is similar to a plea of Guilty and will be treated the same as a Guilty plea.  It is not an admission of guilt, however, so it cannot be used to establish liability in other litigation, such as where personal injury or property damage was involved.

Where pleas of Guilty and No Contest are made, a money judgment (forfeiture) may be entered against you. In some cases, a driver's license suspension or revocation, or other consequences, might apply. You will be given the opportunity to state any mitigating circumstance surrounding the charge. 

If you plead Not Guilty, it means that you feel that you did not commit the violation alleged against you or that the Village cannot prove that you committed the violation.  If you are in doubt as to which plea to enter,  you may plead Not Guilty or ask for an adjournment so you can review your case and make an informed plea at the next court date.  If you plead "Not Guilty", the Village must prove your guilt by clear, satisfactory, and convincing evidence.  In short, the Village must prove that it is highly probably that you committed the offense.
